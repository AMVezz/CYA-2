# Research notes and sources

## Joining undergraduate research labs

* **Participation is widely accessible.** Most undergraduate students at research universities can join faculty‑led labs regardless of major or prior experience【498749047136474†L79-L93】.  Universities actively support undergraduate involvement and some labs recruit freshmen while others prefer students with more coursework【498749047136474†L97-L104】.
* **Motivation and curiosity matter.** When faculty review research assistant applications, they prioritize genuine interest, willingness to learn and consistent commitment over perfect grades【498749047136474†L86-L92】.  Foundational knowledge is helpful, but curiosity and dedication often outweigh transcripts.
* **Explore and network.** Students should identify potential labs by reading department websites and faculty profiles, attend seminars and research symposia, and network with teaching assistants and upperclassmen【498749047136474†L141-L160】.  Upperclassmen provide insight into lab culture and mentoring quality.  The undergraduate research office usually maintains databases of faculty seeking student researchers【498749047136474†L160-L161】.
* **Personalized outreach.** Effective emails to professors are concise, professional and demonstrate familiarity with their research【498749047136474†L169-L181】.  They should mention relevant coursework, express interest in the lab and provide a resume or transcript if requested.

## Contributing to open source

* **Benefits of open source.** Contributing to open source helps you learn, teach and build experience; you can improve skills, meet people, find mentors and build public artifacts that enhance your reputation【689267804346518†L34-L83】.  Open source participation also lets you practise leadership and people skills by collaborating with diverse contributors【689267804346518†L67-L75】.
* **Contributions aren’t limited to code.** Many beginners start by fixing typos, improving documentation or organizing issues; these tasks are often neglected but highly valuable【689267804346518†L94-L100】.  Your first contribution doesn’t need to involve major code changes.
* **Start with familiar projects.** To find a project, think about the software you use regularly and look for improvements you can make【689267804346518†L304-L318】.  Beginners should check a repository’s `/contribute` page or use resources like GitHub Explore, First Timers Only, CodeTriage and Up For Grabs to find entry‑level issues【689267804346518†L324-L339】.
* **Evaluate project health.** Before contributing, verify that the project has an open source license, active commit history, responsive maintainers and a welcoming community【689267804346518†L346-L409】.  Projects with friendly maintainers and active discussion are better for new contributors.
* **Communicate effectively.** When opening an issue or pull request, provide context, explain what you’re trying to accomplish, and do your homework by consulting documentation and past issues【689267804346518†L441-L454】.  Keep messages concise to respect maintainers’ time【689267804346518†L461-L464】.

## Preparing for software internships

* **Start early and apply widely.** The application funnel for software internships is competitive; students often apply to many companies and receive few responses.  Starting the search early and applying to enough positions increases the chances of landing an internship【841212808208285†L128-L138】.
* **Self‑assessment and targeting.** Assess your strengths and weaknesses in programming languages and projects to align internship choices with your skills【841212808208285†L140-L144】.  Make a list of companies whose products you enjoy and search for their internship postings【841212808208285†L146-L149】.
* **Side projects and open source.** Building side projects—such as web applications, mobile apps, hackathon prototypes—or contributing to open source helps differentiate you from other applicants.  Maintaining a well‑documented GitHub profile acts as a living résumé【841212808208285†L180-L204】.
* **Technical interview preparation.** Practice algorithms and data structures in advance because technical interviews are often the biggest barrier for students.  Courses and mentorship programs can provide structured preparation【841212808208285†L166-L176】.
* **Networking.** Building professional connections through alumni, classmates and industry events is critical.  Approximately 70% of jobs come from networking【841212808208285†L207-L215】, so forging relationships can lead to referrals and opportunities.
