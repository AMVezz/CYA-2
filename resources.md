# Research notes and sources

## Joining undergraduate research labs

* **Participation is widely accessible.** Most undergraduate students at research universities can join faculty‑led labs regardless of major or prior experience.  Universities actively support undergraduate involvement and some labs recruit freshmen while others prefer students with more coursework.
* **Motivation and curiosity matter.** When faculty review research assistant applications, they prioritize genuine interest, willingness to learn and consistent commitment over perfect grades.  Foundational knowledge is helpful, but curiosity and dedication often outweigh transcripts.
* **Explore and network.** Students should identify potential labs by reading department websites and faculty profiles, attend seminars and research symposia, and network with teaching assistants and upperclassmen.  Upperclassmen provide insight into lab culture and mentoring quality.  The undergraduate research office usually maintains databases of faculty seeking student researchers.
* **Personalized outreach.** Effective emails to professors are concise, professional and demonstrate familiarity with their research.  They should mention relevant coursework, express interest in the lab and provide a resume or transcript if requested.

## Contributing to open source

* **Benefits of open source.** Contributing to open source helps you learn, teach and build experience; you can improve skills, meet people, find mentors and build public artifacts that enhance your reputation.  Open source participation also lets you practise leadership and people skills by collaborating with diverse contributors.
* **Contributions aren’t limited to code.** Many beginners start by fixing typos, improving documentation or organizing issues; these tasks are often neglected but highly valuable.  Your first contribution doesn’t need to involve major code changes.
* **Start with familiar projects.** To find a project, think about the software you use regularly and look for improvements you can make.  Beginners should check a repository’s `/contribute` page or use resources like GitHub Explore, First Timers Only, CodeTriage and Up For Grabs to find entry‑level issues.
* **Evaluate project health.** Before contributing, verify that the project has an open source license, active commit history, responsive maintainers and a welcoming community.  Projects with friendly maintainers and active discussion are better for new contributors.
* **Communicate effectively.** When opening an issue or pull request, provide context, explain what you’re trying to accomplish, and do your homework by consulting documentation and past issues.  Keep messages concise to respect maintainers’ time.

## Preparing for software internships

* **Start early and apply widely.** The application funnel for software internships is competitive; students often apply to many companies and receive few responses.  Starting the search early and applying to enough positions increases the chances of landing an internship.
* **Self‑assessment and targeting.** Assess your strengths and weaknesses in programming languages and projects to align internship choices with your skills.  Make a list of companies whose products you enjoy and search for their internship postings.
* **Side projects and open source.** Building side projects—such as web applications, mobile apps, hackathon prototypes—or contributing to open source helps differentiate you from other applicants.  Maintaining a well documented GitHub profile acts as a living résumé.
* **Technical interview preparation.** Practice algorithms and data structures in advance because technical interviews are often the biggest barrier for students.  Courses and mentorship programs can provide structured preparation.
* **Networking.** Building professional connections through alumni, classmates and industry events is critical.  About 70% of jobs come from networking, so forging relationships can lead to referrals and opportunities.
